A trend-based, exam-focused word bank for the English paper

How this list was built (read first): The exam-pattern analysis below is drawn from the official UPSC CDS previous-year-paper archive and corroborated against published topic-and-frequency analyses of CDS papers (CDS I & II, c. 2015–2025) and the current AFCAT English pattern. Specific past questions are NOT reproduced or invented. The word list itself is a trend-based curation: words are chosen for their fit with the synonym/antonym pool, recurring one-word-substitution and idiom themes, common root families, and the Barron's high-frequency band that matches CDS difficulty. Treat the whole list as 'high-probability' rather than 'guaranteed to repeat'.
Section 1 — Exam Trend Analysis
1.1  Where vocabulary actually scores marks
In both exams, vocabulary pays twice. Directly, through synonyms, antonyms, idioms and one-word substitution. Indirectly, through cloze tests, fill-in-the-blanks, sentence improvement and reading comprehension, where the right answer turns on knowing a word's exact shade of meaning. So a strong vocabulary lifts 30–40% of the English paper, not just the obvious 'word' questions.

AFCAT is shorter and slightly easier than CDS on average, with negative marking, so accuracy on the high-frequency words below matters more than breadth.
1.4  Difficulty profile
CDS: mostly Moderate, with a steady tail of Difficult words drawn from the same band as Barron's high-frequency GRE list (e.g. abstruse, perfidious, obdurate).
AFCAT: Easy to Moderate; difficult words appear but rarely decide the cut-off.
1.5  Repeated patterns and common traps
Near-synonym trap: in antonym questions, one option is a synonym (not the opposite). Always confirm you are asked for the opposite before choosing.
Shade-of-meaning trap: two options are both 'kind of right'; the test wants the closest, not the merely related word (e.g. 'diminish' vs 'destroy').
Connotation trap: positive vs negative tone (e.g. 'famous' vs 'notorious', 'childlike' vs 'childish').
Root-family trap: options share a root to confuse (e.g. 'credible / credulous / creditable').
Literal-vs-idiom trap: idiom options include the tempting literal meaning.
Spelling/confusable trap (esp. AFCAT): principal/principle, stationary/stationery, etc. — see Section 5.
1.6  Repeated themes (what the words are usually about)
Across years the tested words cluster around: character and conduct (integrity, candour, hypocrite), leadership and discipline (prudent, resolute, complacent), conflict and courage (valour, belligerent, intrepid), praise and criticism (extol, censure), and emotion/temperament (jovial, sullen, irascible). These themes shaped the selection in Section 2.

Section 5 — Commonly Confused Words
These pairs are favourites in spelling, error-spotting and sentence-improvement questions, especially in AFCAT.
Confused pair
Difference
Tip to remember

Affect / Effect
'Affect' (verb) = to influence; 'Effect' (noun) = result
Affect = Action; Effect = End result.

Accept / Except
'Accept' = to receive; 'Except' = excluding
'Ex-' in Except = exclude.

Adapt / Adopt
'Adapt' = adjust to; 'Adopt' = take on as your own
Ada-PT = adjust; ad-OPT = opt to take.

Principal / Principle
'Principal' = head/main; 'Principle' = a rule
The principal is your 'pal'; principle = rule.

Stationary / Stationery
'Stationary' = not moving; 'Stationery' = writing materials
'e' in stationEry = Envelope/pEn.

Complement / Compliment
'Complement' = completes; 'Compliment' = praise
Compl-E-ment = complete; compl-I-ment = praise (I like it).

Counsel / Council
'Counsel' = advice; 'Council' = a group/assembly
CounSEL = adviSE; counCIL = a body.

Eligible / Illegible
'Eligible' = qualified; 'Illegible' = unreadable
'Il-' = not, can't read.

Eminent / Imminent
'Eminent' = famous; 'Imminent' = about to happen
Imm-IN-ent = coming IN soon.

Ingenious / Ingenuous
'Ingenious' = clever; 'Ingenuous' = innocent/naive
-OUS innocent; -IOUS clever idea.

Loose / Lose
'Loose' = not tight; 'Lose' = to misplace/fail
Loose has an extra 'o' = loose.

Desert / Dessert
'Desert' = dry land/abandon; 'Dessert' = sweet dish
Dessert has 2 's' = So Sweet.

'Cite' = quote; 'Site' = place; 'Sight' = vision

Precede / Proceed
'Precede' = come before; 'Proceed' = to go on

Devise / Device
'Devise' (verb) = to plan; 'Device' (noun) = a gadget

Advice / Advise
